  • which timebox and habits techniques did you try?
    • I tried the Timebox technique, where I set up an hour to review the info that was provided (although I went past that hour to finish all the videos). I also implemented the idea of celebrating your accomplishments (I like a silent, "Yay!" with my arms raised in triumph).
  • what did you find interesting?
    • Some of the habits-forming techniques I've heard of before like pomodoro technique and breaking down tasks into smaller pieces. What I found interesting was how systematic human behaviour can really be. These kinds of systems work really well for people who like to be analytical and break down concepts into pieces - for example, recognizing the intrinsic and extrinsic rewards that work for you and how to implement them in your life, or how to break down a larger task like weight lose into the smaller pieces that make that goal achievable. That being said, there's a balance between programming human behaviour with triggers and practice, and honouring our natural rhythms and cycles like one of the last articles explained.
  • what worked for you?
    • Celebrations definintely work for me - taking time to recognize effort goes a long way for me in validating that effort. It makes the to-do list feel less daunting because I know that no matter what I accomplish, I'm a superstar for doing it ;) The pomodoro technique was also good for me, with some variation - I have more concentration in the mornings than afternoons, so I would give myself longer focus blocks in the first half of the day, or not at all and just use the technique for afternoons when I have more difficulty focusing.
  • which will you experiment further with?
    • I liked many of the ideas conveyed from Fogg and from the "Seven Counterinuitive Ways...". I want to try implementing tiny new habits after already-developed habits - and then celebrate them. I also want to "aim to get nothing done", which again makes you feel accomplished when you do complete tasks. I also definitely want to try giving myself less time - I know that I often procrastinate because I know I have enough time, often too much time. But it just means I spend a lot of time sitting around bored, avoiding doing things, and in turn not doing anything fun. If I limit how much time I let myself do a task I need to do, then not only does it force me to get that task completed, it opens up the rest of my time to do hobbies and other fun things! I think that technique will go a long way for me.
